# Continuous paper

Continuous paper is not a neutral carrier. It is the archive surface.

## From page to strip

A normal printer produces pages. This apparatus should produce a strip, a tail, a ledger, or a paper body.

That changes the relation between text and time. The output is not a sequence of finished documents. It is a continuous trace of the system's operation.

## Possible spatial behaviors

The paper can:

- fall into a pile;
- hang from the printer;
- accumulate in a box;
- be pulled across a table;
- become a dated ledger;
- remain partially unreadable;
- be torn and archived daily;
- expose failures, reprints, jams, and interruptions.

No single behavior is fixed yet.

## Form feed

Automatic form feed makes the printer behave like an office device. That may be useful for calibration, but it is not the default artistic behavior.

For continuous archive output, the preferred direction is:

```txt
print fragment -> line feed / spacing -> continue
```

rather than:

```txt
print fragment -> form feed -> eject page
```

## Paper as evidence

The paper should preserve traces of:

- time;
- generation;
- machine rhythm;
- interruption;
- density;
- ribbon wear;
- bad encoding;
- alignment;
- system failure.

Some of these traces may be errors technically and evidence artistically.

## Open questions

- Should visitors be able to read the current output?
- Should the printed archive be touched?
- Should paper be dated in present time, fictional 2047 time, or both?
- Should each day become a bundle?
- Should failed output be marked or left as raw trace?
- Should the paper be preserved after the exhibition?
