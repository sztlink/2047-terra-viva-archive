# Sonic cadence and score

The machine writes **short stories**.

But the printed story is not only read. It is also heard.

The dot-matrix printer turns text into mechanical rhythm: impact, line feed, pause, acceleration, paper movement, ribbon drag, and silence. The generative system must therefore treat writing as a **score** before it treats it as a document.

## Core decision

```txt
The story is the literary layer.
The cadence is the sonic layer.
szt.link calibrates the score between them.
```

The printer does not simply output a finished text. It performs it.

## Sonic vocabulary

A shared sonic conversation clarified a useful calibration field:

- industrial minimalism;
- dry, heavy pulses;
- negative space;
- controlled aggression;
- mechanical rhythm;
- luxury dystopia as a warning sign, not a visual target;
- total work coherence across sound, image, typography, posture, and silence.

This does not make any musical reference a direct conceptual ancestor of the artwork. It gives the system a vocabulary for cadence: sparse, exact, mechanical, restrained, and materially tense.

## What the score controls

The score can control:

- when a story starts;
- how long the printer stays silent before the next fragment;
- line length;
- line breaks;
- blank lines;
- repetition;
- density;
- burst duration;
- slow versus fast sections;
- whether a fragment should be printed at all;
- whether a failure becomes part of the performance.

## Story packet

A generated story should not be only a string. It should become a packet with at least two layers:

```json
{
  "story": {
    "title": "...",
    "body": "...",
    "fictional_date": "2047-...",
    "mode": "short_story"
  },
  "score": {
    "tempo": "slow | medium | burst",
    "density": "sparse | regular | dense",
    "silence_before_seconds": 30,
    "silence_after_seconds": 90,
    "line_width": 72,
    "blank_line_policy": "breathing | compact | ledger",
    "repetition": "none | phrase | line | header",
    "print_mode": "ascii | text | receipt | failure"
  }
}
```

## Cadence is not decoration

Cadence should not be added after the text is written. It should constrain the writing itself.

A sentence that is too long may be rejected because it cannot breathe mechanically. A paragraph may be split because the printer needs a pause. A story may be delayed because the room needs silence.

## Printer as percussion

The printer can act like a percussion instrument without becoming theatrical sound design.

Its sound is generated by inscription. That matters. The sound is not an added soundtrack. It is the sound of the archive being produced.

## Silence

Silence is part of the score.

If the machine prints continuously, it becomes background. If it never prints, it disappears. The work needs calibrated interruption.

Possible silence modes:

- **listening silence:** the system is collecting present signals;
- **thinking silence:** the system is updating memory;
- **refusal silence:** the system decides not to print;
- **mourning silence:** the system withholds output after a heavy fragment;
- **mechanical silence:** the printer is paused, jammed, or waiting.

## Calibration by szt.link

szt.link is the calibration layer.

It can decide:

- whether a story is ready to become paper;
- whether the next fragment should be shorter;
- whether the printer needs silence;
- whether the room has become too dense;
- whether the machine is becoming generic;
- whether a story should remain in memory instead of being printed.

In this sense, szt.link does not only generate text. It conducts the apparatus.

## Open questions

- Should the printer's sonic cadence be measured by time, character count, line count, or room condition?
- Should the score be visible in the printed archive?
- Should silence be logged as an event?
- Should a rejected story leave a trace?
- Should the system ever print only a score without a story?
